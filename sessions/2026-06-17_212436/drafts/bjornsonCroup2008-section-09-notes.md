# Pharmacotherapy — Epinephrine

Nebulised epinephrine provides rapid short-term symptom relief while corticosteroids take effect.

- **Historical context** - In children with severe croup, use of epinephrine reportedly reduced intubation/tracheotomy substantially (historical comparisons); no contemporary RCTs with these hard endpoints exist.
- **vs placebo (3 RCTs)** - Nebulised racemic epinephrine (2.25%) improved croup scores within 10–30 min; a fourth placebo-controlled trial did not show clear benefit but was not well-designed nor well-reported.
- **Objective measures (5 prospective cohort studies)** - Substantial improvement in pathophysiological measures of severity after epinephrine.
- **Duration of effect** - Sustained for ≥1 h; essentially gone within 2 h. Reassuringly, symptoms return to baseline severity on wearing off — not worse than baseline.
- **Discharge safety (5 prospective trials, 253 outpatients treated w/ epinephrine + dexamethasone/budesonide, observed 2–4 h)** - Only 12/253 (5%) returned for care within 48–72 h; only 6/253 (2%) admitted; no adverse outcomes. Safe to discharge if symptoms have not recurred within 2–4 h.
- **One dose: no adverse effects** - No clinically significant increase in heart rate or blood pressure (7 trials, 238 children). Mild side effects most frequently: tachycardia and pallor.
- **Rare adverse event** - One case report: previously healthy child developed ventricular tachycardia and myocardial infarction after 3 doses within 1 h.
- **Racemic vs L-epinephrine 1:1000** - Equally effective and safe (RCT, 31 children, moderate-to-severe croup).
- **Dosing (same dose irrespective of size)** - Racemic epinephrine 2.25%: 0.5 mL in 2.5 mL saline; L-epinephrine 1:1000: 5 mL. Rationale: tidal volume regulates effective dose delivered to airway (from lower-airway aerosol medication data).
