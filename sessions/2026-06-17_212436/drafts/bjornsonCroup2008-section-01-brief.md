# Content Brief — Section 01: Introduction

**Lines:** 1–11 (per outline)

## What to capture

- **Scope**: croup as a common childhood disease; acute onset of distinctive barky cough, stridor, hoarse voice, respiratory distress from upper-airway obstruction
- **Impact on families**: distressing, unusual presentation; predominantly affects young children; symptoms worse in early morning hours
- **Historical context**: before corticosteroids and racemic epinephrine — intubation, tracheotomy, death were typical; treatment evolved from "barbaric methods" (bleeding, leeches) → mist kettles/mist tents → evidence-based corticosteroids + nebulised epinephrine
- **Open questions the paper addresses**: why worse at night? what predisposes to severe vs mild? biannual peak? emerging viral triggers? bacterial tracheitis as emerging complication?
- **Paper's intent**: summarise current published work on epidemiology, diagnosis, and management; propose future research pathways

## Key claims

- Croup is one of the most frequent causes of acute respiratory distress in young children (6 months–3 years, peak at 2 years, ~5% annual incidence)
- Boys > girls (1.4:1)
- Peaks in late autumn (Sept–Dec) in North America; odd-numbered years ~50% higher than even-numbered years (correlates with parainfluenza prevalence)
- Most children (85%) present with mild croup; <1% severe; <5% admitted; 1–3% of admitted require intubation; mortality ~1 in 30,000
- Symptoms usually short-lived: ~60% resolve within 48 h; up to 1 week in a few

## Suggested sub-bullets

- Parainfluenza viruses (types 1 and 3) are the most common cause; also influenza A/B, adenovirus, RSV, metapneumovirus, coronavirus HCoV-NL63
- Laryngeal diphtheria and measles are rare causes in immunised populations
- Pathophysiology: generalised airway inflammation and oedema → subglottic narrowing → barky cough, turbulent airflow, stridor, chest-wall indrawing → further narrowing → hypoxia, hypercapnia, respiratory failure
- Possible mechanisms for night-time worsening: circadian cortisol fluctuations (trough 2300–0400 h)
- Individual susceptibility factors: intrinsic subglottic anatomy, immune response variability, toddler age + small subglottic space
