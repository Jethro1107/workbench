# Bjornson & Johnson — Croup (2008)

## Introduction

- **Croup** — common childhood disease; sudden onset of distinctive barky cough, stridor, hoarse voice, and respiratory distress from upper-airway obstruction
    - Most children (>85%) present with mild, short-lived illness
    - Presentation is unusual and frightening to families; symptoms worse in early morning hours
    - Predominantly affects young children (6 months–3 years, peak at 2 years)

- **Epidemiology** — ~5% annual incidence; boys > girls (1.4:1)
    - Peaks in late autumn (Sept–Dec) in North America; odd-numbered years ~50% higher than even-numbered years (correlates with parainfluenza prevalence)
    - Outcomes: <1% severe; <5% admitted; 1–3% of admitted require intubation; mortality ~1 in 30,000
    - Course: ~60% resolve within 48 h; up to 1 week in some

- **Aetiology** — parainfluenza viruses (types 1 and 3) are most common; also influenza A/B, adenovirus, RSV, metapneumovirus, coronavirus HCoV-NL63
    - Laryngeal diphtheria and measles are rare causes in immunised populations

- **Pathophysiology** — generalised airway inflammation and oedema → subglottic narrowing → barky cough, turbulent airflow, stridor, chest-wall indrawing → further narrowing → hypoxia, hypercapnia, respiratory failure

- **Night-time worsening** — possible role of circadian cortisol fluctuations (trough 2300–0400 h)

- **Individual susceptibility factors** — intrinsic subglottic anatomy, immune response variability, toddler age + small subglottic space

- **Historical context** — before corticosteroids and racemic epinephrine: intubation, tracheotomy, and death were typical outcomes
    - Treatment evolved: barbaric methods (bleeding, leeches) → mist kettles, mist rooms, mist tents → current evidence-based corticosteroids + nebulised epinephrine

- **Paper's intent** — summarise current published work on epidemiology, diagnosis, and management; propose future research pathways
    - Open questions: why worse at night? what predisposes to severe vs mild? biannual peak? emerging viral triggers? bacterial tracheitis as emerging complication?
