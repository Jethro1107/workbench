**Croup** is a common childhood disease characterised by sudden onset of a seal-like barky cough, stridor, hoarse voice, and respiratory distress from upper-airway obstruction. Corticosteroids are firmly established as the treatment of choice across all severity levels; nebulised epinephrine provides rapid short-term relief; and humidified air is definitively ineffective despite >100 years of use. Most children present with mild, self-limited illness, but the minority with severe disease face substantial risk of intubation and death if untreated.

---

## Introduction and Epidemiology

- **Clinical presentation** — sudden onset of distinctive barky cough, stridor, hoarse voice, and respiratory distress resulting from upper-airway obstruction:
    - Predominantly affects children aged 6 months–3 years (peak at 2 years); rare in infants <3 months and adolescents; adults occasionally affected.
    - Symptoms worse during early morning hours; fluctuate w/ agitation vs calm.
    - Usually preceded by non-specific URI symptoms for 12–48 h before barky cough and difficulty breathing develop.
    - Presentation is unusual and frightening to families; >85% of children presenting to emergency are classified as mild; <1% as severe.

- **Epidemiology** — ~5% annual incidence; male/female preponderance 1.4:1:
    - Peaks in late autumn (Sept–Dec) in North America; cases occur throughout the year, incl. summer.
    - Odd-numbered years show ~50% higher admission rates than even-numbered years, closely correlating w/ community parainfluenza prevalence.
    - Outcomes: <5% admitted to hospital; 1–3% of admitted require intubation; mortality ~1 in 30,000.

- **Aetiology** — parainfluenza viruses (types 1 and 3) most common; also influenza A/B, adenovirus, RSV, metapneumovirus, coronavirus HCoV-NL63:
    - Laryngeal diphtheria and measles are rare causes in immunised populations; outbreaks of diphtheric croup reported in Russia and India; measles remains important in non-immunised children (vitamin A reduces secondary infection risk).
    - Whether new pathogens are emerging or improved detection explains the expanding list of associated viruses is unknown; work on a parainfluenza vaccine is ongoing.

- **Pathophysiology** — generalised airway inflammation and oedema of the upper-airway mucosa (larynx, trachea, bronchi) → epithelial necrosis and shedding:
    - Parainfluenza virus activates chloride secretion and inhibits sodium absorption across the tracheal epithelium, contributing to airway oedema.
    - Subglottic narrowing produces the barky cough, turbulent airflow and stridor, and chest-wall indrawing.
    - Further narrowing → asynchronous chest-wall and abdominal movement, fatigue, hypoxia, hypercapnia, and respiratory failure.

- **Night-time worsening** — possible role of circadian fluctuations in endogenous serum cortisol (peak ~0800 h, trough 2300–0400 h); similar mechanisms proposed in asthma (nocturnal airway cooling, GERD, increased tissue inflammation).

- **Individual susceptibility factors** — intrinsically narrower subglottic space; range of inflammatory response to infection; toddler age + small subglottic space + increased viral exposure.

- **Historical context** — before corticosteroids and racemic epinephrine: intubation, tracheotomy, and death were typical outcomes; treatment evolved from barbaric methods (bleeding, leeches) → mist kettles, mist rooms, mist tents → current evidence-based practice.

## Differential Diagnosis

In a child presenting w/ classic signs and symptoms of croup, alternate diagnoses are uncommon. However, clinicians must remain vigilant bc other serious diseases can present w/ stridor and respiratory distress.

- **Bacterial tracheitis** - serious, life-threatening bacterial infection that can arise after an acute viral respiratory-tract infection:
    - Child usually has mild-to-moderate illness for 2–7 days then becomes acutely worse.
    - If febrile, toxic appearance (ie, look unwell and have reduced interaction w/ environment), and do not respond to nebulised epinephrine → consider bacterial tracheitis.
    - Treatment: close airway monitoring, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics; intubation and respiratory support may be needed early when thick tracheal secretions can occlude the airway.
    - Most frequent pathogen: *Staphylococcus aureus*; others: group A streptococcus, *Moraxella catarrhalis*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*; anaerobic bacteria also cultured.
- **Epiglottitis** - now rare due to widespread Hib immunisation:
    - Sudden onset of high fever, drooling, dysphagia, anxiety, preference to sit upright in "sniffing position" (sitting forward w/ head extended) to open airway.
    - Cough does NOT have the characteristic barky sound of croup.
    - Most important aspect of treatment: maintenance of a secure airway by a clinician highly skilled in airway management.
- **Foreign-body aspiration** - onset usually sudden, no prodrome, no fever (unless secondary infection):
    - Hoarseness and barky cough usually absent; dysphagia may be present; stridor variable.
    - Usually clear history of ingestion.
- **Peritonsillar / retropharyngeal abscess**:
    - Presents w/ dysphagia, drooling, stridor, dyspnoea, tachypnoea, neck stiffness, unilateral cervical adenopathy.
    - Lateral neck radiograph: posterior pharyngeal oedema and retroflexed cervical vertebrae.
- **Acute angioneurotic oedema / allergic reaction**:
    - Rapid onset at any age; dysphagia, stridor, possible urticarial rash.
    - History of allergy or previous attacks.
- **Laryngeal diphtheria** - now rare in immunised populations; outbreaks reported in Russia and India:
    - Gradual onset over 2–3 days; low-grade fever.
    - Membranous pharyngitis on exam; hoarseness and barky cough present along w/ dysphagia and inspiratory stridor.
    - History of inadequate immunisation.

> n.b. Key distinguishing feature of croup: the characteristic barky cough. Key red flags suggesting alternative diagnoses: toxic appearance, drooling, dysphagia, high fever, absence of barky cough.

## Diagnosis and Ancillary Testing

- **Clinical diagnosis** — Croup is diagnosed on clinical features alone; key features include acute onset of a seal-like barky cough, stridor, hoarseness, and respiratory distress:
    - Children may have fever up to 40°C; however, they should not drool nor appear toxic.
- **Laboratory tests** — Not needed to confirm typical croup; if judged necessary, defer if child is in respiratory distress:
    - Rapid antigen tests and viral cultures do not aid routine acute management.
- **Radiographs** — Not recommended in typical croup responding appropriately to treatment; not indicated if clinical picture suggests epiglottitis or bacterial tracheitis:
    - If diagnosis uncertain: anteroposterior and lateral soft-tissue neck radiograph can support alternative diagnosis.
        - Epiglottitis: thickened epiglottis and aryepiglottic folds.
        - Retropharyngeal abscess: bulging soft tissue of posterior pharynx.
        - Bacterial tracheitis: ragged tracheal contour or membrane spanning trachea.
    - n.b.: radiographs can also be completely normal in these alternative diagnoses.
    - If radiographs justified by atypical picture → child must be closely monitored during imaging by skilled personnel with appropriate airway equipment, bc airway obstruction can worsen rapidly.
- **Cardiorespiratory monitoring** — Continuous pulse oximetry indicated in severe croup only; not necessary in mild cases:
    - Children without severe croup may have low oxygen saturation from intrapulmonary involvement → ongoing clinical assessment important.

## Assessment of Severity

- **Severity determined by clinical assessment** — objective methods for assessing respiratory distress are either impractical or insensitive across the full range of disease severity:
    - Clinical scores (e.g., croup scores) are useful in research trials but **none has been shown to enhance routine clinical care**; not reliable when used by a wide range of clinicians.
    - Features useful in routine clinical assessment (per the management algorithm figure, adapted from Johnson D et al., Alberta Medical Association clinical practice guidelines):
        - *Mild* — no stridor or significant chest-wall indrawing at rest → may discharge home.
        - *Moderate* — stridor and chest-wall indrawing at rest without agitation.
        - *Severe* — stridor and indrawing of the sternum associated with agitation or lethargy.

> n.b. severity assessment is integral to the treatment algorithm — corticosteroid use, epinephrine, observation duration, and admission decisions all flow from severity classification.

## General Care

- **Comfort and minimising agitation** — consensus that children with croup should be made as comfortable as possible; agitation causes substantial worsening of symptoms:
    - Sitting the child comfortably in the lap of a parent or caregiver is usually the best way to lessen agitation.
    - *Evidence base* is primarily consensus rather than RCT data.
- **Oxygen therapy** — no published evidence that oxygen should be administered to children showing signs of respiratory distress, yet widespread consensus indicates it is beneficial:
    - Can generally be administered without causing agitation via a plastic hose with the opening held within a few centimetres of the nose and mouth — referred to as *blow-by oxygen*.
    - Low-tech and well-accepted approach.
- **Mild cases** — no specific interventions beyond comfort, positioning, and blow-by oxygen are required.

## Humidified Air

- **Ineffectiveness** - Treatment of croup with humidified air is not effective, despite its long history of use; humidification of air is neither completely benign nor does it improve respiratory distress:
    - Systematic review (3 RCTs, 135 children w/ mild-to-moderate croup in emergency settings): no difference in croup score after humidified air treatment.
    - Later RCT (140 children w/ moderate-to-severe croup, three arms: traditional standard humidified blow-by oxygen; 40% humidified oxygen; or 100% humidified oxygen with particle size generated to target the larynx): no difference in croup score, treatment with epinephrine or dexamethasone, or admission between groups.
    - Measurement of humidified blow-by oxygen showed it did not raise humidity above ambient room air — effectively serving as a placebo arm.
- **Potential harms** - Hot humidified air → scald injuries; mist tents → disperse fungus and moulds unless properly cleaned; mist tents are cold and wet and separate child from parent → causes agitation → worsens symptoms.
- **Bottom line** - Mist tents should not be used; humidification provides no clinical benefit.

## Heliox

- **Mechanism** - Helium is an inert low-density gas w/ no inherent pharmacological or biological effects; heliox reduces airflow turbulence through a narrowed airway (lower density vs nitrogen), thereby decreasing respiratory distress in severe croup:
    - Not shown to offer greater improvements than standard treatments; difficult to use in unskilled hands; limited fractional concentration of inspired oxygen in hypoxic children.
- **Evidence vs racemic epinephrine** - Prospective RCT, 29 children w/ moderate-to-severe croup, all received humidified oxygen + IM dexamethasone; heliox and racemic epinephrine → similar improvements in croup score over time.
- **Evidence vs oxygen-enriched air** - Prospective RCT, double-blind, 15 children w/ mild croup; trend toward greater improvement in croup score w/ heliox vs oxygen-enriched air, but not statistically significant.
- **Bottom line** - Insufficient evidence to recommend general use in children w/ severe croup; a niche intervention for skilled settings only.

## Pharmacotherapy — Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids have a long history of use in children with croup; evidence for their effectiveness is now clear and robust across all severity levels.

- **Effectiveness — meta-analyses (Table 1)**:
    - Griffin (2000) — 8 studies, 574 patients: nebulised corticosteroid vs placebo → improvement in croup score at 5 h (RR 1.48 [1.27–1.74]); reduction in admission (RR 0.56 [0.42–0.75]).
    - Kairys (1989) — 10 studies, 1286 patients: oral/IM corticosteroid vs placebo → improvement at 12 h (OR 2.25 [1.66–3.06]), at 24 h (OR 3.19 [1.70–5.99]); reduction in endotracheal intubation (OR 0.21 [0.05–0.84]).
    - Russell (2004) — 31 studies, 3736 patients: oral/IM/nebulised corticosteroid vs placebo → improvement in croup score at 6 h (weighted mean difference –1.2 [–1.6 to –0.8]); reduction in return to medical care (RR 0.5 [0.36–0.70]); reduction in length of stay (weighted mean difference 12 h [5–19]); reduction in nebulised epinephrine use (risk difference 10% [1–20]).

- **Effectiveness — selected RCTs (Table 2)**:
    - Bjornson (2004) — 720 children w/ mild croup, oral dexamethasone vs placebo: return to medical care within 7 days reduced (7% vs 15%, p<0.001); parents of children treated w/ dexamethasone lost less sleep and reported less stress.
    - Johnson (1998) — 144 children w/ moderate-to-severe croup, nebulised budesonide or IM dexamethasone vs placebo: admission reduced (35% vs 67%, p<0.001).
    - Tibballs (1992) — 70 children w/ respiratory failure or intubated, oral prednisolone vs placebo: duration of intubation reduced (median 98 vs 138 h, p<0.003); 7-fold lower risk of reintubation.
    - Geelhoed (1995) — 80 children w/ moderate-to-severe croup, nebulised budesonide or oral dexamethasone vs placebo: duration of admission reduced (12/13 h vs 20 h, p<0.03).

- **Severe croup outcomes** — children w/ severe croup and impending respiratory failure treated w/ corticosteroids have ~5-fold reduction in intubation rate; if intubated, remain ventilated ~one-third less time and have 7-fold lower risk of reintubation vs untreated patients.

- **Mild croup outcomes** — children w/ mild croup treated w/ corticosteroids are 50% less likely to return for medical care and lose 30% less sleep during illness; parents report less stress; small but clinically important societal economic benefits (total saving of CAN$21 per child). Benefits arise irrespective of symptom duration or illness severity.

- **Route of administration (Table 3)** — oral or IM is either equivalent or superior to inhalation:
    - Nebulised vs oral/IM: no difference in duration of admission, croup score, or admission rate across multiple RCTs.
    - Oral vs IM: no difference in symptom resolution, return for medical care, admission, or further treatment (Rittichier 2000; Donaldson 2003).
    - IM dexamethasone vs oral betamethasone: no difference in croup score, admission, time to symptom resolution, or return for medical care.
    - Addition of inhaled budesonide to oral dexamethasone in admitted children: no additional advantage.
    - *Practical note*: oral dexamethasone is associated w/ less vomiting than oral prednisone; for persistent vomiting, inhaled or IM route preferable; in hypoxia, oral/IM absorption may be impaired → inhaled route allows concurrent oxygen or racemic epinephrine administration.

- **Dosing (Table 4)** — conventional dexamethasone dose is 0.60 mg/kg; whether 0.15 or 0.30 mg/kg is equally effective remains unresolved:
    - Four RCTs comparing different oral dexamethasone doses (0.15, 0.30, 0.60 mg/kg) found no significant difference in primary outcomes; however, none was designed as a non-inferiority study and all had small sample sizes.
    - Meta-analysis (6 studies of children admitted to hospital): higher dose of hydrocortisone equivalents → higher proportion responding vs placebo; however, all study designs differed — possibility of bias.
    - Since most children's croup symptoms resolve within 72 h and the anti-inflammatory effect of dexamethasone lasts 2–4 days, a second dose would seem unnecessary in most children.
    - Standard dose: oral dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg (one dose).

- **Safety** — no adverse effects definitively associated w/ corticosteroid use in children w/ croup to date; theoretical concerns include:
    - Varicella exposure: conflicting evidence on risk of disseminated disease or bacterial superinfection in immunocompetent children; FDA, AAP, and AAAAI advise caution in children exposed to varicella virus.
    - Potential but unproven complications: bacterial tracheitis, pneumonia, gastrointestinal bleeding.
    - Viral shedding: no evidence that corticosteroid use prolongs viral shedding.

## Pharmacotherapy — Epinephrine

Nebulised epinephrine provides rapid short-term symptom relief while corticosteroids take effect.

- **Historical context** - In children with severe croup, use of epinephrine reportedly reduced intubation/tracheotomy substantially (historical comparisons); no contemporary RCTs with these hard endpoints exist.

- **vs placebo (3 RCTs)** - Nebulised racemic epinephrine (2.25%) improved croup scores within 10–30 min; a fourth placebo-controlled trial did not show clear benefit but was not well-designed nor well-reported.

- **Objective measures (5 prospective cohort studies)** - Substantial improvement in pathophysiological measures of severity after epinephrine.

- **Duration of effect** - Sustained for ≥1 h; essentially gone within 2 h. Reassuringly, symptoms return to baseline severity on wearing off — not worse than baseline.

- **Discharge safety (5 prospective trials, 253 outpatients treated w/ epinephrine + dexamethasone/budesonide, observed 2–4 h)** - Only 12/253 (5%) returned for care within 48–72 h; only 6/253 (2%) admitted; no adverse outcomes. Safe to discharge if symptoms have not recurred within 2–4 h.

- **One dose: no adverse effects** - No clinically significant increase in heart rate or blood pressure (7 trials, 238 children). Mild side effects most frequently: tachycardia and pallor.

- **Rare adverse event** - One case report: previously healthy child developed ventricular tachycardia and myocardial infarction after 3 doses within 1 h.

- **Racemic vs L-epinephrine 1:1000** - Equally effective and safe (RCT, 31 children, moderate-to-severe croup).

- **Dosing (same dose irrespective of size)** - Racemic epinephrine 2.25%: 0.5 mL in 2.5 mL saline; L-epinephrine 1:1000: 5 mL. Rationale: tidal volume regulates effective dose delivered to airway (from lower-airway aerosol medication data).

## Pharmacotherapy — Other Drugs

No controlled trials support use of analgesics/antipyretics, antibiotics, antitussives, decongestants, or short-acting β2 agonists in croup.

- **Analgesics / antipyretics** - Reasonable for reduction of fever or discomfort; no RCT evidence but widely accepted as appropriate symptomatic support.

- **Antibiotics** - Most croup is viral; superinfections (bacterial tracheitis, pneumonia) are rare (<1 per 1000 croup cases), making prophylactic antibiotics unreasonable.

- **Antitussives and decongestants** - No physiologically rational basis exists for use in upper-airway obstruction; should not be administered.

- **Short-acting β2 agonists** - No clear rationale for use in upper-airway disease; croup is not lower-airway bronchospasm; should not be administered.

> n.b. Practice variation documented across 21 Alberta hospitals — these unproven drug categories are nonetheless sometimes used despite no evidence. Established therapies (corticosteroids, epinephrine) have robust RCT evidence; these adjunct categories do not.

## Indications for Admission and Discharge

Little published evidence guides admission decisions; practice relies heavily on expert opinion and clinical pathways.

- **Evidence base** — retrospective cohort, Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne, 527 children with persistent stridor at rest (before routine corticosteroids): persistent sternal indrawing at ED presentation → 6% risk of endotracheal intubation; without sternal or chest-wall indrawing → recovered rapidly without specific treatment.

- **Corticosteroid effect on admission** — reductions in admissions first noted at 3 h, with increasing differences up to 10 h after dexamethasone vs placebo; rate of admission in dexamethasone group was half that of placebo. Implies at least 3 h, ideally up to 10 h observation after corticosteroid reduces admission rates.

- **Clinical pathway effect** — mandating 6 h observation after corticosteroid before admission decision → substantial reduction in admissions (Chin et al.).

- **Alberta Medical Association Clinical Pathway (figure)** — severity-stratified management algorithm:
    - *Mild* (no stridor or significant chest-wall indrawing at rest) — oral dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg; parent education on anticipated course, signs of respiratory distress, when to return; discharge without further observation.
    - *Moderate* (stridor and chest-wall indrawing at rest without agitation) — nebulised epinephrine + oral dexamethasone; observe; if improved (no indrawing, no stridor at rest) by 4 h, discharge home.
    - *Severe* (stridor and sternal indrawing with agitation or lethargy) — nebulised epinephrine + dexamethasone; contact PICU; consider admission if poor response or recurrent distress after ≥4 h of steroid.

- **Discharge criteria** — no chest-wall indrawing, no stridor at rest; responsiveness to corticosteroid ± epinephrine; 2–4 h observation without recurrence of severe symptoms; parents educated on course of illness, signs of respiratory distress, when to seek care.

## Conclusion

Corticosteroids are firmly established as the treatment of choice for children with croup after 50 years of controversy; they have greatly reduced health-care use and enhanced outcomes across all severity levels. Epinephrine has sufficient data to support its role in rapid short-term symptom relief until corticosteroids take effect. Conversely, after >100 years of use, humidified air is definitively ineffective. Heliox remains a niche intervention with insufficient evidence for general recommendation. Several mysteries remain unexplained — why symptoms worsen at night, what predisposes some children to severe vs mild disease, the cause of the biannual peak, whether new viral triggers are emerging, and whether bacterial tracheitis is an emerging complication. Future exploration of these questions could yield novel and even more effective treatments or vaccines.
