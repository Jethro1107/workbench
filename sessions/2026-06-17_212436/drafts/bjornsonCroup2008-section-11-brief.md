# Content Brief — Section 11: Indications for Admission and Discharge

**Lines:** 83–87 (per outline)

## What to capture

Little published evidence guides admission decisions; practice relies heavily on expert opinion and clinical pathways.

### Evidence base

- Retrospective cohort (527 children admitted to Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne, with persistent stridor at rest, before routine corticosteroids):
  - Persistent sternal indrawing at ED presentation → 6% risk of intubation
  - Without sternal/chest-wall indrawing → recovered rapidly without specific treatment
- Dexamethasone vs placebo: reductions in admissions first noted at 3 h, increasing up to 10 h after treatment → suggests **at least 3 h, ideally up to 10 h observation** after corticosteroid treatment reduces admission rates
- Clinical pathway (6 h observation mandated after corticosteroid before admission) → substantial reduction in admissions (Chin et al.)

### Alberta Medical Association Clinical Pathway

- Management algorithm operationalises severity-based decisions
- Mild: discharge after oral dexamethasone + parent education
- Moderate: nebulised epinephrine + dexamethasone; observe; discharge if good response
- Severe: nebulised epinephrine + dexamethasone; contact PICU; consider admission if poor response or recurrent distress

### Discharge criteria

- No chest-wall indrawing, no stridor at rest
- Responsiveness to treatment with corticosteroids ± epinephrine
- 2–4 h observation without recurrence of severe symptoms
- Parents educated on course of illness, signs of respiratory distress, when to return

## Suggested sub-bullets

- Core principle: severity drives management; observation allows treatment effect to manifest before admission decision
- Observation period is therapeutic (corticosteroids take hours to work)
- Parent education is a key component of safe discharge
