# Content Brief — Section 03: Diagnosis and Ancillary Testing

**Lines:** 43–48 (per outline)

## What to capture

- Croup is a **clinical diagnosis**; key features: acute onset of seal-like barky cough, stridor, hoarseness, respiratory distress
- Children may have fever up to 40°C but should NOT drool or appear toxic
- Laboratory tests NOT needed to confirm typical croup; if needed, defer if child is in respiratory distress
- Rapid antigen tests and viral cultures do not aid routine acute management
- Radiological studies NOT recommended in typical croup that responds appropriately to treatment
- Radiographs NOT indicated if clinical picture suggests epiglottitis or bacterial tracheitis
- If diagnosis uncertain: anteroposterior and lateral soft-tissue neck radiograph can help support alternative diagnosis
  - Epiglottitis: thickened epiglottis, thickened aryepiglottic folds
  - Retropharyngeal abscess: bulging soft tissue of posterior pharynx
  - Bacterial tracheitis: ragged tracheal contour or membrane spanning trachea
  - BUT: radiographs can also be completely normal in these conditions
- If radiographs justified by atypical picture → child must be closely monitored during imaging with skilled personnel and appropriate airway equipment
- Cardiorespiratory monitoring (continuous pulse oximetry) indicated in severe croup only; not necessary in mild cases
- Children without severe croup could have low oxygen saturation from intrapulmonary involvement → ongoing clinical assessment important
