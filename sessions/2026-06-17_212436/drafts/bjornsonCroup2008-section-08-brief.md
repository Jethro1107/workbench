# Content Brief — Section 08: Pharmacotherapy — Corticosteroids

**Lines:** 69–73 (per outline)

## What to capture

Corticosteroids have the strongest evidence base in croup management.

### Effectiveness (meta-analyses)

- **Severe croup / intubated children**: ~5-fold reduction in intubation rate; if intubated, ventilated for ~1/3 less time; 7-fold lower risk of reintubation
- **Moderate-to-severe croup**: ~12 h reduction in ED/hospital length of stay; 10% absolute reduction in nebulised epinephrine use; 50% reduction in return visits and admissions
- **Mild croup**: 50% less likely to return for medical care; 30% less sleep lost; parents report less stress; savings of CAN$21 per child; benefits arise irrespective of symptom duration or severity

### Route of administration

- Oral or IM is equivalent or superior to inhaled (nebulised budesonide)
- Adding inhaled budesonide to oral dexamethasone in admitted children: no additional advantage
- Oral vs IM dexamethasone: no difference in symptom resolution, return visits, admission, further treatment
- IM dexamethasone vs oral betamethasone: no difference in croup score, admission, time to resolution, return visits
- Dexamethasone is the most studied oral corticosteroid; associated with **less vomiting than prednisone** (practical advantage)
- For persistent vomiting: inhaled or IM route preferable
- For severe respiratory distress: oral administration may not be tolerated
- In hypoxia: oral/IM absorption may be impaired → inhaled route allows concurrent oxygen/epinephrine delivery

### Dosing

- Conventional dose: 0.60 mg/kg dexamethasone
- Lower doses (0.30 mg/kg, 0.15 mg/kg) have been studied; four RCTs show no significant difference in outcomes between doses
- One dose is likely sufficient (symptoms resolve within 72 h; anti-inflammatory effect of dexamethasone lasts 2–4 days)
- Systematic review (6 studies of hospitalised children): higher hydrocortisone-equivalent doses → greater response vs placebo

### Risks

- **Varicella exposure**: conflicting evidence on risk of complicated varicella (disseminated disease, bacterial superinfection); FDA/AAP advise caution in exposed children
- Potential unproven complications: bacterial tracheitis, pneumonia, GI bleeding
- Bacterial tracheitis: proposed link to immune dysfunction (unproven)
- In stem-cell transplant recipients: higher corticosteroid dose associated with parainfluenza pneumonia
- GI bleeding unlikely in healthy children; more concern in PICU, intubated, high-dose patients
- **No adverse effects definitively associated with corticosteroid use in croup in otherwise healthy children**
