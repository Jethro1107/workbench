# Content Brief — Section 09: Pharmacotherapy — Epinephrine

**Lines:** 74–78 (per outline)

## What to capture

Nebulised epinephrine provides rapid short-term symptom relief while corticosteroids take effect.

### Historical context

- Use of epinephrine in severe croup reportedly reduced intubation/tracheotomy substantially (historical comparisons)

### Evidence

- **vs placebo (3 RCTs)**: nebulised racemic epinephrine (2.25%) improved croup scores within 10–30 min; one trial not well-designed/reported → unclear benefit
- **Objective measures** (5 prospective cohort studies): substantial improvement in pathophysiological measures after epinephrine
- Effect sustained for **at least 1 h**; essentially gone within 2 h
- As effect wears off, symptoms return to **baseline** (on average) — not worse than baseline (reassuring)
- **Combined data (5 prospective trials, 253 outpatients treated with epinephrine + dexamethasone/budesonide, observed 2–4 h)**: only 12/253 (5%) returned for care within 48–72 h; only 6/253 (2%) admitted; no adverse outcomes
- Safe to discharge after 2–4 h observation if symptoms have not recurred

### Safety

- One dose: no associated adverse effects; no clinically significant increase in heart rate or blood pressure
- Critical review (7 trials, 238 children): only mild side effects — most frequently tachycardia and pallor
- **Exception**: one case report of ventricular tachycardia and myocardial infarction after 3 doses within 1 h in a previously healthy child (rare)
- Racemic epinephrine vs L-epinephrine 1:1000: equally effective and safe (RCT, 31 children, moderate-to-severe croup)

### Dosing practice

- Same dose used in all children irrespective of size (traditional practice)
- Racemic epinephrine 2.25%: 0.5 mL in 2.5 mL saline
- L-epinephrine 1:1000: 5 mL
- Rationale: tidal volume regulates effective dose delivered to airway (from lower-airway aerosol medication data)
