# Content Brief — Section 02: Differential Diagnosis

**Lines:** 33–42 (per outline)

## What to capture

This section lists alternative diagnoses that can present with stridor and respiratory distress, distinguishing them from uncomplicated viral croup.

## Key entities to characterise

1. **Bacterial tracheitis**
   - Serious, life-threatening bacterial infection following acute viral respiratory-tract infection
   - Child usually has mild-to-moderate illness for 2–7 days, then acutely worsens
   - Febrile, toxic appearance, does not respond to nebulised epinephrine → consider bacterial tracheitis
   - Treatment: close airway monitoring, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, possible intubation
   - Most frequent pathogen: *Staphylococcus aureus*; also group A strep, *Moraxella catarrhalis*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*; anaerobes also cultured

2. **Epiglottitis**
   - Now rare due to widespread Hib immunisation
   - Sudden onset high fever, drooling, dysphagia, anxiety, preference for upright "sniffing position"
   - Cough does NOT have the characteristic bark
   - Most important treatment: maintenance of a secure airway by skilled clinician

3. **Foreign-body aspiration**
   - Sudden onset, no prodrome, no fever (unless secondary infection)
   - Hoarseness and barky cough usually absent; dysphagia may be present
   - Usually clear history of ingestion

4. **Peritonsillar / retropharyngeal abscess**
   - Dysphagia, drooling, stridor, dyspnoea, tachypnoea, neck stiffness, unilateral cervical adenopathy
   - Lateral neck radiograph: posterior pharyngeal oedema, retroflexed cervical vertebrae

5. **Acute angioneurotic oedema / allergic reaction**
   - Rapid onset at any age; dysphagia, stridor, possible urticarial rash
   - History of allergy or previous attacks

6. **Laryngeal diphtheria**
   - Gradual onset over 2–3 days; low-grade fever; membranous pharyngitis on exam
   - History of inadequate immunisation

## Suggested sub-bullets

- The panel of differential diagnoses is extensive but all are uncommon in children with *classic* croup presentation
- Clinicians must remain vigilant because serious diseases can present with stridor
- Key distinguishing feature of croup: characteristic barky cough
- Key red flags: toxic appearance, drooling, dysphagia, high fever, no barky cough → consider alternatives
